Step-by-Step Roadmap

Zero se pehli sale tak — actual kaam

Har step realistic hai — Jhargram ke liye, as is.

1
Learn / Research
Visit the Jhargram Craft Cluster in town center and speak with 2-3 existing sal leaf plate makers (contact through Jhargram District Industries office, free), attend the weekly Thursday haat at Jhargram Main Market, call UNIDO field office in Jhargram to understand cluster support and artisan networks — all doable this week at ₹0
2
Source Raw Materials
Source dried sal leaves directly from forest collection groups in Jhargram taluk (connect via UNIDO or District Industries office) at ₹8-12 per kg. First batch of 1000 leaves costs ₹3000-4000. Supplier area: Jhargram town and surrounding forest villages (Bandwan, Porahat blocks)
3
Produce / Partner
Partner with 5-10 tribal women artisans on contract basis (₹100-150 per day wage + materials cost) OR rent a 300 sq ft basic unit in Jhargram town (₹3000-5000/month) and hire 3-4 experienced folding workers. Setup cost for unit + drying racks + folding tables: ₹80,000-1,50,000. Production capacity: 200-300 units/day with 5-6 workers
4
Brand & Register
Register Udyam (free, online, 15 min), open a current account in your name or cooperative society (required for wholesale contracts). No GI registration needed yet (not GI-tagged). Costs: ₹0 Udyam, ₹2000-3000 bank account setup, ₹500 business license from Jhargram Municipality
5
First Sale
First 100 units: contact 2-3 local canteen suppliers directly (reference from District Industries), expect ₹45,000-60,000 revenue in month 1 at ₹450-600 per unit. Simultaneously list 50 units on Amazon Artisan (free listing, 35% commission). First order likely 50-100 units within 2-3 weeks once listed
6
Scale
At ₹10L/month revenue: hire a dedicated packaging and quality check person (₹8000/month), establish standing order contracts with 3-5 institutional buyers (requires compliance officer, ₹5000/month), attend East India craft fairs (Kolkata, Siliguri annually), expand to printed/branded plates (₹2L equipment investment), integrate 15-20 artisans as primary suppliers

PM Vishwakarma
₹15,000 toolkit subsidy + ₹5 lakh collateral-free loan at 5% interest for artisans and small craft producers

SFURTI (Scheme of Fund for Regeneration of Traditional Industries)
₹50-100 lakh cluster development grant for existing craft clusters, covers training and equipment

West Bengal Artisan Credit Scheme
up to ₹5 lakh loan at 4% interest for craft entrepreneurs, collateral-free

25% subsidy on production equipment, ₹50,000-2,00,000 range for craft businesses in notified ODOP clusters

Direct approach to institutional canteen suppliers and caterers in Jhargram, Kolkata (contact via District Industries referral), list on Amazon Artisan and Ajoby (free listings), attend regional craft fairs in Kolkata and Siliguri. First bulk order usually comes within 2-4 weeks — expect 100-500 units at ₹400-600/unit from a canteen supplier or eco-product retailer.
Limited export potential currently — no GI tag and market is regional. Can export to Nepal, Bangladesh as eco-products, but margins are low (30-35% FOB) and logistics are complex. Better to focus on Eastern India wholesale and D2C first. If exporting, you need IEC registration (₹5000-8000) and compliance with food-contact standards if selling as food service products.
Downside: raw material (sal leaves) is dependent on forest supply and seasonal availability. Market is mostly B2B and seasonal (high in Puja, festivals). If demand drops, you can pivot to value-added products (printed/branded plates at higher margins) or shift to gifting and corporate bulk orders. Exit option: sell existing artisan contracts to another producer or shift capital to complementary craft (stone carving, pottery). UNIDO cluster provides some risk-sharing through collective marketing.

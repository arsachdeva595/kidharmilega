Step-by-Step Roadmap

Zero se pehli sale tak — actual kaam

Har step realistic hai — Alirajpur ke liye, as is.

1
Learn / Research
Visit Alirajpur district headquarters and connect with tribal cooperatives (Alirajpur Tribal Development Society, Dhar District Cooperative). Study Mahua harvest patterns (May-June peak season) at forest villages like Udaygarh and Sondwa. Interview 5-10 traditional Mahua producers to understand fermentation, distillation, and quality standards.
2
Source Raw Materials
Source fresh Mahua flowers directly from tribal gatherers in Alirajpur forests (₹80-120/kg) or from collector cooperatives at district mandi. First batch of 500kg flowers costs ₹40,000-60,000. Establish relationships with 3-4 suppliers for season-long contracts. Source bottles from Indore glass suppliers (₹15-25 per unit for premium bottles).
3
Produce / Partner
Partner with established Mahua distillers in Sondwa or Udaygarh villages for first 6 months (contract manufacturing at ₹200-300 per liter). Alternatively, set up small batch distillery (15,000L capacity) in industrial area near district headquarters with basic fermentation tanks and distillation setup. Hire 2-3 experienced tribal producers as technical consultants.
4
Brand & Register
Register business as MSME/Udyam (online, free). Apply for IEC (Import-Export Code) from DGFT office in Indore for export readiness. Register trademark with IP India (₹5,000-8,000). Obtain FSSAI license for food processing (₹5,000-12,000). Get alcohol manufacturing license from Excise Department, Madhya Pradesh. Design label emphasizing tribal heritage, organic sourcing, and GI-aspirational positioning.
5
First Sale
Launch on Amazon, Flipkart, JioMart with premium spirits category (₹800-1,500 per bottle). List on niche platforms: TheArtisanMart, Rajasthanicraft.com, heritage food marketplaces. Approach premium bars and restaurants in Indore, Bhopal, and Ahmedabad with B2B pricing. Join Alirajpur ODOP network for government e-commerce promotion. Start with 100 units first month targeting lifestyle influencers.
6
Scale
After 6 months, expand to 5-7 product variants (Mahua butter, flower tea, cosmetic oil). Establish direct export relationships with EU importers through trade shows (India Food Forum, Gulfood). Scale production to 50,000L annual capacity. Build direct B2B partnerships with 20-30 premium hotels and restaurants. Invest in cold-chain logistics and export-grade packaging (₹30L). Launch brand storytelling content on Instagram and YouTube highlighting tribal producer stories.
Government Schemes

Woh paisa jo tujhe pata nahi tha

🏛
PMEGP (Prime Minister Employment Generation Programme)
up to ₹25L loan (80% subsidy for SC/ST entrepreneurs, 15-35% subsidy for others) for food processing unit setup
🏛
SFURTI (Scheme of Fund for Regeneration of Traditional Industries)
up to ₹25L grant for cluster development, skill training, common facility centers in Alirajpur
🏚
Madhya Pradesh ODOP Scheme
dedicated government support for product promotion, quality certification, export subsidies, and brand development for Alirajpur Mahua
🏚
Mukhya Mantri MSME Loan Guarantee Scheme
zero-interest collateral-free loans up to ₹50L for registered MSMEs in food processing sector
Pehla step: udyamregistration.gov.in — free, 10 minute. Bina iske koi scheme apply nahi hogi.

Marketing

Ye marketing ideas try karna mat bhulna

  • Partner with heritage and lifestyle influencers (50K-500K followers) on Instagram for 'tribal tourism' content series — visiting Mahua forests, meeting tribal producers, distillation process videos. Budget: ₹2-4L per quarter.
  • Launch 'Artisan Story' video series on YouTube and Facebook showcasing individual tribal Mahua harvesters and their family histories. Create 12 short documentaries (3-5 min each) emphasizing cultural heritage and sustainable practices.
  • Target premium spirits communities on Facebook and WhatsApp groups (craft spirits enthusiasts, heritage product collectors). Offer exclusive 'founder batches' and limited edition seasonal releases. Run 'Mahua storytelling' webinars with cultural historians and tribal elders.
